Cats ( Felis catus)

The domestic cat (Felis catus) is a tiny carnivorous animal.It is the only domesticated species in the Felidae family and is sometimes referred to as the domestic cat to distinguish it from the family's wild members.A cat can be categorized as a house cat, a farm cat, or a feral cat, the latter of which roams freely and avoids human interaction.Humans cherish domestic cats for their companionship and their capacity to destroy rats.

Domestication

The earliest documented evidence for the domestication of an African wildcat (F. lybica) was discovered near a human Neolithic cemetery at Shillourokambos, southern Cyprus, about 7500-7200 BC. Cats' anatomy and behavior have changed relatively slightly after domestication, and they are still capable of living in the wild. Several natural traits and habits of wildcats may have pre-adapted them for domestication as pets. Their tiny size, sociable nature, obvious body language, love for play, and relatively high intellect are among these characteristics.

Characteristics

Size

The European wildcat has a larger cranium and shorter bones than the domestic cat.It has a head-to-body length of around 46 cm (18 in) and a height of about 23-25 cm (9-10 in), with tails that are about 30 cm (12 in) long. Males are physically bigger than females.

Skeleton

Cats have seven cervical vertebrae (as do most mammals), 13 thoracic vertebrae (as do humans), seven lumbar vertebrae (as do humans), three sacral vertebrae (as do most mammals, while humans have five), and a variable number of caudal vertebrae in the tail (humans have only three to five vestigial caudal vertebrae, fused into an internal coccyx)

Skull

Cat skulls are unique among animals in that they have exceptionally wide eye sockets and a powerful specialized jaw. Cats' jaws are equipped with teeth designed for killing animals and shredding flesh. When a cat overpowers its victim, it uses its two large canine teeth to administer a devastating neck bite, placing them between two of the prey's vertebrae and severing its spinal cord, causing irreversible paralysis and death.

Claws

Cats have claws that can extend and retract.The claws are enclosed with skin and fur around the toe pads of the paw in their usual, relaxed state. This maintains the claws sharp by reducing wear from ground contact and enables for quiet surveillance of prey. The forefeet's claws are usually sharper than the hindfeet's. Cats may lengthen their claws on one or more paws voluntarily. Claws may be extended for hunting or self-defense, climbing, kneading, or increased traction on soft terrain.

Balance

During a fall from a great height, a cat uses its excellent sense of balance and flexibility to twist its body and right itself to land on its feet. The cat righting reflex is the name given to this reaction. If a cat has enough time to do so, as is the case in falls of 90 cm (2 ft 11 in) or more, it will always right itself in the same way The "fallen cat problem" has been studied to see how cats can correct themselves after falling.
